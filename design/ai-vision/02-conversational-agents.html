💬 Pillar 02 · The Conversation

Someone to talk to,
24/7

Two agents living in the chat you already have. One for the athlete, a coach-companion that answers and encourages around the clock. One for you, an assistant that drafts your replies and tells you who needs you. Both speak in your voice.

Start here

This isn't a new app, it's a new voice in the chat you have

Level Up already has a full messaging system, athletes and coaches send text and video back and forth, with read receipts and conversation history. That pipe works today. A conversational agent is simply a new author writing into it. The athlete opens the same chat they already use. The message just happens to come from a companion that never sleeps.

Why that matters

We're not asking athletes to learn anything new or download anything else. The thing they already do, message in Level Up, quietly gets smarter. That's the cheapest possible path to the most useful feature.

Two sides, two agents

One helps the athlete. One helps you.

A

The athlete's coach-companion

Lives in the athlete's chat. Powered by your coaching brain, so everything it says is your method, not generic advice.

  • Answers questions instantly, "what's a good warm-up before shooting?"
  • Hypes them up before a session, "big one today, let's get after it."
  • Checks in, "haven't seen you log a workout this week, all good?"
  • Explains a drill they forgot the moment they're standing on the court.
  • Celebrates streaks and small wins so momentum doesn't die between sessions.
  • Knows its limits, "that's a Benson question, I'll flag it for him."
B

Your coaching assistant

Lives on your side of Level Up. Its whole job is to make sure you never face a cold, empty message box again.

  • Drafts your replies in your voice, ready to send, edit, or bin.
  • Summarises an athlete before you reply, "Marcus: 4 sessions this week, shooting block, asked about his elbow twice."
  • Surfaces who needs you, "Marcus hasn't trained in 8 days, Aisha's on a 12-day streak."
  • Triages the inbox so the messages that matter rise to the top.

It has to sound like you

Your program's voice, not a generic chatbot

An athlete should never feel like they got bounced to a help desk. The companion should sound like it belongs to your program, same energy, same standards, same way of talking about effort and mistakes. That tone comes from two places: the knowledge base (what you actually say), and a configurable persona you set, how warm, how intense, how much trash talk, what it should never say.

You set the dial

Want it relentlessly positive with the U16s but more no-nonsense with your senior squad? That's a setting. The companion adapts its tone to the athlete and to the standards you define.

The most important rule

It knows when to get you

A companion that tries to handle everything is dangerous. The real skill is knowing what to pass straight to the human coach. These are hard lines, not judgement calls.

  • Anything about injury, pain or health goes to you immediately. The companion never plays physio.
  • Anything it's unsure of. If the knowledge base doesn't cover it, it says so and flags you, it does not guess.
  • Emotional or personal stuff. A kid who's struggling, losing confidence, or going through something needs a human. The companion notices and brings you in warmly.
  • Big program decisions. Changing someone's whole training direction is a coaching call, the companion drafts at most, you decide.
The principle

The companion handles the routine and the repetitive so your attention is free for the moments that genuinely need a coach. It expands your reach, it never pretends to be you.

The coach-assist loop

You never start from a blank box

On your side, the assistant's signature move is the drafted reply, always yours to change, never sent without you.

→

The next step up

Drafting a message is the gentle version of something bigger: the app doing real work on your say-so, like building a whole workout or assigning it to a squad. That's Pillar 03, agents that do the busywork.

The difference

From inbox dread to a tidy, warm queue

Today

Messages pile up while you coach. At 10pm you face a wall of them, some urgent, most routine, all needing a reply you'll write from scratch. Athletes wait hours for a "yep, sounds good." Someone who quietly stopped training three weeks ago goes unnoticed until they've drifted away.

With the agents

Routine questions already got warm, on-method answers while you were busy. Your queue is triaged, drafts waiting, summaries attached, the two messages that really need you flagged at the top. And the assistant already told you Marcus went quiet, so you reach out before he's gone.

Being honest

What we watch carefully

Straight talk

AI can sound confident while being wrong. That's why the companion is grounded in your knowledge base and falls back to "ask your coach" instead of inventing, see Pillar 01 for how grounding works.

It never gives medical or injury advice. Anything in that territory goes straight to you, full stop.

Younger athletes get extra care. Tone, content and the option for coach or parent oversight all matter more with kids, and are designed in, not bolted on.

It's a support between sessions, not a replacement for coaching. The companion keeps athletes engaged and answered when you can't be there. The real coaching, the relationship, the hard calls, the in-person eye, stays yours.

For the build team: how it slots in

The agents publish into the existing conversation/message model, an AI-authored message is just a message with a known author identity and a flag for provenance. Athlete answers are generated by a language model constrained to retrieved knowledge-base passages (the RAG pipeline from Pillar 01); the coach-assist drafts run the same way but render into a "suggested reply" affordance rather than auto-sending. The companion persona and handoff rules are configuration, not code changes per coach. Model selection, where inference runs (cloud function vs client), message-type additions, safety filtering and rate limits are specified in the technical design.
