🧠 Pillar 01 · The Foundation

Your coaching brain,
always on

Everything you know about basketball lives in your head and gets handed out one conversation at a time. This is how we capture it once, and let the app share it with every athlete, in your voice, forever.

The problem worth solving

Your knowledge doesn't scale, and it walks out the door

You have spent years learning how to teach a clean jump shot, when to push conditioning and when to back off, the three cues that finally make a kid stop dropping their elbow. That knowledge is the whole value of your coaching, and right now it has one delivery method: you, in person or in a message, saying it again.

So the same answer gets repeated for the fortieth time. A new athlete gets a thinner version of what a veteran got, because you only have so many hours. And if you ever stepped back, most of it would simply leave with you. The bottleneck isn't your effort, it's that there's only one of you.

The move

Capture your coaching once into a knowledge base the app can draw on. Then any athlete asking "how do I stop getting stripped on the drive?" gets an answer pulled from your drills and your cues, not generic internet fitness advice, and it works at 6am or 2am whether you're awake or not.

What the knowledge base holds

Everything that makes your coaching yours

Not a generic textbook. The specific, hard-won stuff that lives between your ears.

🏀 Drills & progressions

Your ball-handling ladder, your shooting workout for a kid who's plateaued, what comes before what and why.

🗣️ Your cues

"Load the legs, don't push the arms." "Slide, don't cross your feet on defence." The exact words you use that actually land.

📐 Rules of thumb

When to add weight, how many days off after a hard week, what soreness is fine and what isn't.

❓ The FAQs

The questions every athlete asks. "Why aren't my shots falling?" "How do I get a quicker first step?" Answered once, reused forever.

🎯 Your philosophy

How you think about effort, mistakes, confidence, the long game. The stuff that makes athletes want to play for you.

📋 Your existing content

The exercise descriptions and coach notes already sitting in Level Up are knowledge too. They get pulled in automatically.

The real challenge

Getting it in has to be effortless

Here's the honest part: a knowledge base is only as good as what's in it, and the reason most "capture your expertise" tools fail is that capturing is a chore nobody does. So the whole design centres on making it cost you almost nothing.

  • Talk, don't type. Record a voice memo after a session, "today I had three kids struggling with their pull-up, here's what I told them" - the app transcribes it and files it. Two minutes in the car becomes permanent coaching content.
  • Mine what you've already said. You've sent hundreds of chat messages to athletes. With your permission, the app can learn from them - that's coaching you already delivered, now reusable.
  • Use what's already in the app. Every exercise description and coach note you've written is pulled in with zero extra work.
  • Quick brain-dump prompts. Once in a while the app asks one good question, "what's your go-to fix for a flat shot?", and your answer becomes a piece of the brain.
  • Import what you've got. Old training PDFs, a Google Doc of drills, notes on your phone, drop them in and they're absorbed.
Design principle

If capture takes real effort, it won't happen and the feature dies. So capture rides along with things you already do, talking after sessions, messaging athletes, writing workouts, rather than asking you to sit down and "document your knowledge."

How it answers

A fast assistant who has read everything you've ever said

Picture an assistant who has memorised your entire coaching library. When an athlete asks a question, that assistant instantly finds the few pages most relevant, then writes the answer using only what you actually teach. That's the whole trick.

For the curious (and the build team): what's actually happening

This pattern is called RAG - retrieval-augmented generation. In plain terms: your knowledge is broken into small passages and indexed by meaning (not just keywords) in a vector store. When a question comes in, the system finds the passages closest in meaning, then hands those passages to a language model with an instruction like "answer this using only the coach's material, and if it's not covered, say so." The model writes fluent prose, but it's anchored to your content rather than making things up.

Because the answer is grounded in retrieved passages, it stays in your method and we can show which notes an answer came from. Full architecture, model choice and data design live in the technical design.

The difference

From trapped to always-on

Today

An athlete's question sits unanswered until you're free. A beginner gets a quick reply, a thinner version of what you'd say with time. The brilliant explanation you gave Marcus last month is gone, lived once, never reused. And it all depends on you being reachable.

With the coaching brain

The question gets a full, on-method answer in seconds, at any hour. Every athlete gets your best version, not your rushed one. The explanation you gave once now serves everyone who asks something similar. And it keeps working when you're coaching, asleep, or on holiday.

Being honest

What it can't do

Straight talk

It only knows what it's been told. Ask it something you've never covered and it should say "I'm not sure, that's one to ask your coach" rather than invent an answer. Good in, good out, thin in, thin out, which is exactly why low-effort capture matters so much.

It needs you to seed and curate it. Early on you'll want to review answers and correct the odd one. That tuning is quick, and it's how the brain comes to sound exactly like you. Think of it as training an assistant, not flipping a switch.
