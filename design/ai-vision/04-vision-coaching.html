The dream

Feedback on the rep nobody saw.

The reps that build a player aren't the ones in front of you. They're the hundreds done alone, in a driveway, in an empty gym, at 7am before school. Right now those reps happen in the dark - good form and bad form rehearsed exactly the same, with no one to say "fix the elbow".

How a phone "sees" a jump shot

Two pieces, explained plainly

There's no magic here, just two well-understood steps stacked together. Worth understanding both, because they're different jobs.

1

Pose detection

Finding the body in the picture. The app looks at each frame of the video and locates the key points of the body - shoulders, elbows, wrists, hips, knees, ankles - then joins them into a moving stick figure, a skeleton.

Think of those wireframe overlays you see on TV when they break down an athlete's technique. That's pose detection. It answers: where is the body, right now?

2

Action analysis

Reading the movement over time. Once you have the skeleton moving frame by frame, you can interpret it. Is this a jump shot or a defensive slide? Is the form good? How many reps? What was the tempo, the range of motion, the left-right symmetry?

This is the coaching layer. It answers: what did they do, and how well? This is where your standards get encoded.

The short version

Pose detection draws the skeleton. Action analysis reads what the skeleton is doing and grades it against how you'd coach it. One finds the body, the other judges the movement.

Not just fixing mistakes

What a seeing app can do

Correction is the obvious one. But once the app can read movement, a lot opens up.

🔢 Rep counting

No more guessing or self-reporting. The app counts the reps it actually sees - and knows which were clean.

📈 Progress over time

"You got 2cm higher on your vertical this month." "Your shooting elbow is tighter than in April." Hard proof that the work is working.

🎉 Encouragement

Catch the good rep, not just the bad one. "That's your best landing all week." Motivation lands hardest when it's specific and instant.

🏀 Movement recognition

Knows a jump shot from a defensive slide from a crossover, so feedback is matched to what they're actually drilling.

⚖️ Symmetry & tempo

Left side versus right, how fast the movement fired, whether range of motion held up as fatigue set in.

🩹 Injury-risk flags

Spot patterns linked to injury - like knees caving inward on landing - early, and surface them gently before they become a problem.

The idea that ties it to everything else

It doesn't replace your eye. It points it.

This is the heart of it, and it's the same thesis as the whole vision: AI gives the coach reach. Here's exactly how that works for form.

The app gives encouragement and basic feedback on every single rep - the thing you could never do at scale, because you can't watch a hundred driveway clips a night. But it also does something quieter and just as valuable: it triages. Most reps are fine, and the app handles those itself. The handful where something looks genuinely off - a landing that screams injury risk, a shooting form that's drifting - those get flagged and queued at the top for your eyes.

Today

Twelve form videos sit unwatched in your queue. You get to two of them, maybe, on a good week. The athlete who filmed a perfect set gets no reply. The athlete quietly grooving a dangerous landing pattern gets no warning. Your attention is spread by the order things arrived, not by what matters.

With an eye on every rep

All twelve athletes got instant feedback the moment they filmed. Your queue now holds the two clips that actually need expert judgement, sorted with the concerning one first. You spend your review time exactly where it counts, and nobody trained in the dark.

The reach, made concrete

One coach, every athlete's home reps, every night. Not by working more, but by letting the app handle the easy 90% and aim your expertise at the 10% that's worth it. That's the whole vision in a single feature.

Privacy is a feature, not a footnote

The video stays on the phone.

We're asking young athletes to film themselves. That carries real responsibility, and we treat it as a first-class part of the design - not a risk to paper over later. Handled right, it's also a reason families will trust Level Up over anything else.

The key technical choice: the body tracking runs on the athlete's own phone. Modern phones can do pose detection on-device, which means the raw video never has to leave the handset to be analysed. The phone watches the rep, works out the skeleton and the feedback locally, and only the abstract result - a coaching tip, a rep count, a flag - travels anywhere, and only when it should. The footage of a child in their bedroom or driveway stays exactly where it was filmed.

Our privacy stance

On-device by default. Consent up front. The athlete owns their data. These aren't compliance boxes - they're how we earn the right to be in a kid's training.

  • Explicit consent first. Nothing is analysed until the athlete (and, for minors, a parent) has clearly opted in.
  • On-device by default. Pose detection runs locally on the phone wherever the device allows it.
  • Raw video stays local. The footage doesn't need to be uploaded to be analysed, so by default it isn't.
  • Parental consent for minors. Younger athletes get extra protection built in, not bolted on.
  • The athlete controls their data. They choose what's shared with the coach and what stays private to them.
  • Delete anytime. Clips and analysis can be wiped on request, fully and for good.

Most fitness apps quietly ship your video to a server. Doing the opposite - keeping it on the phone - is harder to build, and it's exactly why parents will be comfortable letting their kid use this.

Straight talk

What it can and can't do

This is the most ambitious pillar, so it deserves the most honesty about its edges.

Honest limits
  • It's guidance, not diagnosis. The app coaches form. It does not diagnose injuries or medical conditions, and it never replaces a doctor or physio when something hurts.
  • One camera has limits. A single phone, filming from one angle, can't see everything in true 3D. Camera angle, lighting and framing all affect how well it reads a movement.
  • Accuracy varies by movement. A clean squat or a jump-shot release is easier to judge well than a fast, complex, contact-heavy action.
  • It complements you, never substitutes. For anything subtle or anything to do with pain or injury risk, the call is yours, and a professional's - the app's job is to surface it, not settle it.
  • We start narrow. The first version handles a few well-defined movements done really well - think squat, jump shot, landing mechanics - not "every basketball movement on day one". We earn breadth by getting depth right first.

For the build team

Under the hood

Benson can skip this. For the people building it, here's the shape.

The on-device pose pipeline

Pose estimation runs on-device using a mobile pose model - Google ML Kit Pose Detection or MediaPipe Pose (BlazePose) - both of which give ~33 body landmarks per frame at real-time framerates on modern phones. The app already records form-check clips, so the capture path exists; this adds a per-frame landmark extraction step that runs locally on the captured (or live) video.

On-device matters for two reasons at once: it's the privacy guarantee (raw video never leaves the handset), and it's the cost guarantee (no per-minute cloud vision bill, no video egress, no GPU server to keep warm). The expensive, sensitive part runs for free on hardware the user already owns.

Skeleton to feedback - the coaching layer

The landmark stream (a time series of joint positions) feeds the action-analysis layer. Early versions are best served by rule-based / heuristic analysis per movement: e.g. for a landing, compute knee-over-ankle alignment and flag valgus past a threshold; for a jump shot, measure elbow angle and release consistency; rep counting via peak-detection on a key joint's trajectory. These rules are where Benson's coaching standards get encoded explicitly and transparently.

Heavier action recognition (classifying which movement is being performed, or learned form-quality scoring) can start on-device with small models and, only where genuinely needed and with consent, fall back to a server for the harder classification - sending landmark data, not raw video. Movement classification, thresholds, the flag/triage rules, and the progress-tracking schema are specified in the technical design.
