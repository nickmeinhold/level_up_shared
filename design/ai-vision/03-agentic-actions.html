The leap from the last pillar

A companion talks. An agent does.

Pillar 02 gave you something that can answer. This is the step where the app can act: actually put a workout together, line it up for an athlete, queue a drill, draft a program. The difference between "here's what I'd suggest" and "done, ready for your okay".

And here's the important part: an agent that takes action is only safe and only useful if it stays on a leash you hold. So the whole design of this pillar is built around one promise. Nothing leaves your hands without your tap. The agent prepares; you decide.

The shape of every action

The agent never acts then tells you. It always drafts, shows you, and waits. You are the green light, every time. Think of it as the keenest assistant you've ever had, who happens to never need a break, and who never sends anything in your name without checking first.

In your words, on the court

Things you'd actually ask for

Real requests, in the language you already use. The app handles the assembly; you handle the call.

🎯 "Build Marcus a 3-week shooting block"

The agent pulls catch-and-shoot, off-the-dribble and free-throw drills from your library, paces them across three weeks, and hands you a full draft program to tweak or approve.

👥 "Assign yesterday's session to the U16s"

It finds the session, lists every athlete in the U16 group, and shows you exactly who's about to receive it before a single notification goes out.

🔥 "Bump conditioning for anyone who trained 4+ times this week"

It scans training history, finds the athletes who've earned a harder week, and drafts the adjustment for your sign-off.

🛡 "Who hasn't done a defensive drill in two weeks?"

It surfaces the list, and offers to queue a defensive slide or close-out drill for each. You decide which ones get it.

📅 "Draft next month's program for the beginners"

A full month of progressive sessions - ball-handling fundamentals through to first set plays - drafted from your beginner library for you to shape.

✍️ "Reply to everyone who asked about Saturday"

It drafts a personalised reply to each athlete in your voice. You read them, edit any you want, and release them - or not.

The part that matters

Steps 1 and 2 save you an hour. Steps 3 and 4 keep it unmistakably your coaching. We never collapse the four into "the app just did it" - the preview and the tap are the whole point.

The leash, in detail

Not all actions are equal

Some actions only affect a draft on your screen. Others reach an athlete. The riskier the action, the more explicit the approval. The agent knows the difference.

What the agent doesRiskWhat you have to do
Suggest drills, search your library, summarise an athlete's weekLowNothing - it's just showing you information
Draft a workout or a program (lives only on your screen)LowNothing to approve until you choose to use it
Draft replies to athletes (held, not sent)MediumRead and release each one - nothing sends on its own
Assign a workout to an athlete or a groupHighExplicit tap, with the full recipient list shown first
Send a message to an athlete in your nameHighExplicit tap per message, always reviewable, never automatic

The rule of thumb is simple. If it only touches your screen, the agent gets on with it. If it touches an athlete, you tap first. Always.

Straight talk

Where we hold the line

Honest limits

An agent that takes action is powerful, which is exactly why we build it carefully.

  • Approval gates are non-negotiable for anything an athlete will see. There is no "skip approval" setting for athlete-facing actions, by design.
  • The agent stays inside guardrails. It can only take actions from a fixed, known list - it can't invent new powers for itself or reach outside the app.
  • We start with drafting, the safe end. The first version only ever prepares drafts. Acting-on-approval comes once drafting has earned your trust.
  • Every action is logged. You can always see what the agent did, when, and on whose say-so - a clear audit trail, not a black box.
  • It never messages athletes unsupervised. Not now, not later. A message in your name always passes your eyes first.

For the build team

Under the hood

Benson can skip this. For the people building it, here's the shape.

How the agent actually takes actions

The agent uses tool-use (function calling): the model is given a fixed set of callable tools (draftWorkout, assignWorkout, queueDrill, draftReply, listAthletes, and so on) and chooses which to call to satisfy the request. Crucially, the tools are an allow-list - the agent cannot do anything that isn't a registered tool, so its blast radius is bounded by design rather than by hope.

Each tool carries a risk classification (read-only, draft, athlete-facing). Read-only and draft tools execute immediately and return their result to the screen. Any athlete-facing tool does not execute - it returns a proposed action object that the UI renders as a preview with an explicit approve control. The write only happens after the coach's tap, in a separate confirmed call.

Every executed action is written to an audit log (who, what, when, approved-by) so the trail is queryable. The approval UX, the tool schema, the allow-list, and where the agent loop runs (a Cloud Function, not the client) are specified in the technical design.
